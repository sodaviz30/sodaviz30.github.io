The Risky Nightlife:
Analysis of Crimes in SF during the Late Hours

As the city's lights dim and the streets grow quiet, a different rhythm takes hold — one measured not in footsteps, but in incident reports. This investigation traces the temporal fingerprint of crime across San Francisco's neighborhoods, asking whether darkness truly invites disorder, or whether the data tells a more nuanced story.

Background

Proteting yourself against crime in San Fransisco

The city San Francisco is celebrated for its vibrant culture, iconic skyline, and restless energy, the passage from day to night brings a subtle but meaningful shift. As the sun dips behind the Pacific and neighborhoods transition from bustling daytime activity to the glow of nightlife, patterns of movement, behavior, and interaction begin to change. Streets that once carried commuters and tourists take on new roles—hosting late-night diners, bar crowds, service workers, and those navigating the city after dark.

This project explores over two decades of crime data in San Francisco, spanning from 2003 to 2025, with a specific focus on how incidents are distributed across time—particularly during late-night hours. But this data does contain information about what kind of incident took place, and when and where it took place. Although crime rates are generally falling, it is still necessary to be aware of your surroundings. Rather than assuming that darkness inherently breeds disorder, the analysis seeks to uncover whether crime truly intensifies at night, or if the reality is shaped by more complex social, economic, and environmental factors. By examining temporal trends alongside neighborhood-level variation, this investigation aims to identify recurring patterns: Are certain types of crime more likely to occur after midnight? Do specific districts experience sharper increases during late hours? And how have these dynamics evolved over time? At its core, this study asks a simple but compelling question:

"Does crime increase during nightlife hours?, and how does this affect perceived safety?"

To understand nightlife sf crime, we will analyze the temporal distribution of incidents across different those 4 categories assault, motor vehicle theft, larceny theft og robbery and map these trends onto the city's diverse neighborhoods.

Crime Incidents Across the Week

"Do certain days carry more risk, or are patterns of crime more consistent than we expect?"

From weekday commutes to weekend nightlife, the rhythm of the week shapes how people move through the city. These shifts may also influence when and where crime occurs. By analyzing incidents across each day of the week, this section explores whether crime peaks during weekends, declines midweek, or follows more subtle and unexpected patterns, as illustrated in Figure 1.

As seen in Figure 1, crime levels differ significantly between weekdays and weekends. Larceny theft dominates overall, ranging from 27,748 incidents on Monday to a peak of 41,477 on Saturday. Assaults increase from 9,953 on Monday to 15,889 on Saturday. Motor vehicle thefts rise from 8,597 on Monday to 10,963 on Saturday, while robberies increase from 4,371 on Monday to 5,708 on Saturday. These trends show that weekends experience notably higher crime, likely reflecting increased social activity, nightlife, and movement across the city.
